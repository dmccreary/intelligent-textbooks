What if your textbook evolved itself — the same way the most advanced AI systems do?

That's the central question in a working paper I'm releasing today: "Evolving Pedagogy: Applying Darwin-Gödel-Machine Principles to Self-Improving Intelligent Textbooks."

The Darwin-Gödel-Machine (DGM) is a landmark 2025 system from Sakana AI that improves its own code by replacing formal proof with something much more practical: empirical evidence. It doesn't need to prove a change is better — it just runs the experiment and keeps what works.

My paper argues we can apply exactly this principle to educational content.

Instead of modifying code, we evolve MicroSims — small interactive simulations embedded in intelligent textbooks. Instead of coding benchmarks, we use learning analytics: pre/post assessment gains, Bloom's-level advancement, retention, and engagement depth. And instead of a single best solution, we maintain an archive of variant MicroSims — because a design that underperforms for high-school learners today may be the perfect stepping stone for adult learners tomorrow.

The paper proposes:

- A direct mapping between DGM mechanisms and textbook architecture
- A composite fitness function built from xAPI learning analytics
- The concept dependency graph as a structured evolutionary search space
- Two deployment phases — from anonymous web analytics to identity-bearing LRS streams
- A concrete JSON schema standard for MicroSim performance data

The core insight: we don't need to prove a MicroSim is pedagogically optimal. We need only measure whether students learn better from one variant than another — and let the archive evolve.
